Is FTL travel necessary to get to the stars in a 6 day creation worldview?

The concept of Faster-Than-Light (FTL) travel arises from the limitation imposed by the speed of light in traditional physics. According to Einstein's theory of relativity, nothing with mass can travel at or faster than the speed of light in a vacuum (approximately 299,792 kilometers per second or about 186,282 miles per second). Given the immense distances between stars and galaxies, even at the speed of light, interstellar travel within a human lifetime would be impractical. However, whether FTL travel is necessary depends on the goals and constraints of space exploration. Here are some considerations:

Time Dilation: As an object approaches the speed of light, time dilation occurs, which means time passes more slowly for the moving object compared to a stationary observer. While this could potentially allow for longer journeys from the perspective of the traveler, it doesn't eliminate the challenge of extremely long travel times from an external perspective.

Space-Time Warping: Theoretical physics allows for the possibility of warping or bending space-time, which could theoretically enable faster travel between distant points. Concepts like wormholes or Alcubierre drives are speculative ideas that involve manipulating space-time to achieve faster travel.

Relativistic Travel: Even without reaching the speed of light, interstellar travel at a significant fraction of the speed of light could be explored. This would still involve overcoming substantial engineering and technological challenges but might be more feasible than achieving FTL speeds.

In summary, while the speed of light poses significant challenges for traditional forms of space travel, whether FTL travel is necessary depends on the chosen approach to interstellar exploration.

There are theoretical concepts and ideas that scientists and researchers are exploring, but as of my last knowledge update in January 2022, no practical and proven method for FTL travel exists within the framework of our current understanding of physics.
